Interpreting an idea presented in persuasive texts

Insufficient evidence of interpretation.
Identifies an issue presented in one or more texts.
Explains how an issue is presented in one or more texts.
Analyses how an issue is represented in one or more texts.
Compares and contrasts how an issue is represented in more than one text.
Analyses, integrates and reframes an idea presented in more than one text.

Justifying a response to persuasive texts

Insufficient evidence of response to a persuasive text.
Describes personal responses to a persuasive text.
Selects and uses evidence from one or more texts to justify responses to the text/s.
Analyses supporting evidence and includes examples to substantiate personal responses to one or more texts.
Expresses their own interpretations of, and responses to, texts.

Analysing rhetorical (persuasive) devices

Insufficient evidence
Identifies some rhetorical devices used in one or more persuasive texts.
Explains how rhetorical devices are used in one or more texts to influence audiences.
Compares and contrasts how rhetorical devices work in two texts to persuade audiences to accept points of view on an issue.
Compares and contrasts persuasive strategies in two texts, specifically in relation to positioning the target audiences.

Analysing text structures in persuasive texts

Insufficient evidence
Identifies the structure(s) of one or more persuasive texts, such as describing the sequence of ideas.
Explains how the structures of one or more texts assist in representing information to readers.
Analyses how the structures of one or more texts (such as problem/solution, cause/effect etc.) persuade readers to accept a viewpoint on an issue.
Compares and contrasts the structures of two texts and analyses how they persuade readers to accept a viewpoint on an issue.

Analysing language features in persuasive texts

Insufficient evidence
Identifies language features and specific vocabulary choices in a text.
Analyses how language features and vocabulary choices shape representations of an issue to influence readers’ interpretations in at least one text.
Compares and contrasts how language features and vocabulary choices in two texts shape representations of an issue to influence readers’ interpretations.
Compares and contrasts the overall effectiveness of language features and vocabulary choices, including register, to influence readers and distinguish the work of two authors.

About Rubrics

Rubrics are ‘rules’ to guide teacher judgements as they assess student work. They establish observable evidence, in the form of what a student can say, do, make or write, and they promote reliable and consistent in judgement-based assessments.

This interactive rubric presents each strand separately with its own set of rubrics. It is intended to help teachers assess how well student learning is demonstrated without feeling locked into a singular column. While some strands may be interdependent, a student at one level on one strand is not automatically at a comparable level on other strands.
